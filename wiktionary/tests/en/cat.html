Etymology 1

From Template:Inh, [Template:M used with invalid code 'enm'. See documentation.]  catte, from Template:Inh, [Template:M used with invalid code 'ang'. See documentation.]  catte, from Template:Inh. Template:Rel-top

The Germanic word is generally thought to be from Template:Der (c. 350, Palladius), from Template:Der (c. 75 A.D., Martial),[1] from an Template:Der language. This would roughly match how domestic cats themselves spread, as genetic studies suggest they began to spread out of the Near East / Fertile Crescent during the Neolithic (being in Cyprus by 9500 years ago,[2][3] and Greece and Italy by 2500 years ago[4][5]), especially after they became popular in Egypt.[2][3] However, every proposed source word has presented problems. Adolphe Pictet[6] and many subsequent sources refer to Barabra (Nubian) [Template:M used with invalid code 'onw'. See documentation.] and "Nouba" (Nobiin) [Template:M used with invalid code 'fia'. See documentation.]  kadīs as possible sources or cognates,[7] but M. Lionel Bender says the Nubian word is a loan from Template:Noncog.[8] Jean-Paul Savignac suggests the Latin word is from an Egyptian precursor of

Noun

Template:En-noun

  1. An animal of the family Felidae:
    • Template:Quote-book
    [improper synthesis?]
    1. A domesticated subspecies (Felis silvestris catus) of feline animal, commonly kept as a house pet. Template:Defdate
      • Template:RQ:WBsnt IvryGt
        At twilight in the summer there is never anybody to fear—man, woman, or cat—in the chambers and at that hour the mice come out. They do not eat parchment or foolscap or red tape, but they eat the luncheon crumbs.
      [improper synthesis?]
    2. Any similar animal of the family Felidae, which includes lions, tigers, bobcats, etc.
      • 1977, Peter Hathaway Capstick, Death in the Long Grass: A Big Game Hunter's Adventures in the African Bush, St. Martin's Press, 44.
        I grabbed it and ran over to the lion from behind, the cat still chewing thoughtfully on Silent's arm.
      • 1985 January, George Laycock, "Our American Lion", in Boy Scouts of America, Boy's Life, 28.
        If you should someday round a corner on the hiking trail and come face to face with a mountain lion, you would probably never forget the mighty cat.
      • 2014, Dale Mayer, Rare Find. A Psychic Visions Novel, Valley Publishing.
        She felt privileged to be here, living the experience inside the majestic cat [i.e. a tiger]; privileged to be part of their bond, even for only a few hours.
  2. A person:
    1. Book:En (edit | talk | history | links | watch | logs) A spiteful or angry woman. Template:Defdate
      • 1835 September, anonymous, "The Pigs", in The New-England Magazine, Vol. 9, 156.
        But, ere one rapid moon its tale has told, / He finds his prize — a cat — a slut — a scold.
      [improper synthesis?]
    2. An enthusiast or player of jazz.
      • Template:Quote-song
    3. Book:En (edit | talk | history | links | watch | logs) A person (usually male).
      • Template:Quote-song
      • 1973 December, "Books Noted", discussing A Dialogue (by James Baldwin and Nikki Giovanni), in Black World, Johnson Publishing Company, 77.
        BALDWIN: That's what we were talking about before. And by the way, you did not have to tell me that you think your father is a groovy cat; I knew that.
      • Template:Quote-song
      • Template:Quote-song
      [improper synthesis?]
    4. Book:En (edit | talk | history | links | watch | logs) A prostitute. Template:Defdate
      • 1999, Carl P. Eby, Hemingway's Fetishism. Psychoanalysis and the Mirror of Manhood, State University of New York Press, 124.
        “Tell me. Willie said there was a cat in love with you. That isn't true, is it?” “Yes. It's true,” Hudson corrects her, letting her think that by “cat” he means prostitute.
  3. Book:En (edit | talk | history | links | watch | logs) A strong tackle used to hoist an anchor to the cathead of a ship.
    • 2009, Olof A. Eriksen, Constitution - All Sails Up and Flying, Outskirts Press, 134.
      Overhaul down & hook the cat, haul taut. Walk away the cat. When up, pass the cat head stopper. Hook the fish in & fish the anchor.
  4. Book:En (edit | talk | history | links | watch | logs) Template:Non-gloss definition cat-o'-nine-tails.
    • Template:Quote-book
  5. Book:En (edit | talk | history | links | watch | logs) A sturdy merchant sailing vessel (now only in "catboat").
  6. Book:En (edit | talk | history | links | watch | logs) The game of "trap and ball" (also called "cat and dog").
    1. The trap of the game of "trap and ball".
  7. Book:En (edit | talk | history | links | watch | logs) The pointed piece of wood that is struck in the game of tipcat.
  8. Book:En (edit | talk | history | links | watch | logs) A vagina, a vulva; the female external genitalia.
    • Template:Quote-book
    • Template:Quote-book
    • Template:Quote-book
  9. A double tripod (for holding a plate, etc.) with six feet, of which three rest on the ground, in whatever position it is placed.
  10. Book:En (edit | talk | history | links | watch | logs) A wheeled shelter, used in the Middle Ages as a siege weapon to allow assailants to approach enemy defences.
